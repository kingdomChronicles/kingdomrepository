Sacred Writing

"Every writings must be evaluated based on the truths they contain."

From the Nativity of the Virgin Mary to the death of patriarch Saint Joseph

Chapter 1

The Holy Family Among Robbers

1At some distance from the road by which they were travelling, a light glimmered through the dark-ness.
2It proceeded from a hut belonging to a gang of robbers, who had hung a light on a neighboring tree, thus to allure travelers.
3The road too, here and there, was broken by pits over which cords with little bells were stretched.
4The ringing of these bells gave notice to the robbers of the presence of luckless wayfarers.
5All on a sudden, I saw a man with about five comrades surrounding the Holy Family.
6All were actuated by wicked intentions.
7But when they looked at the Child, I saw a glittering ray like an arrow penetrating the heart of the leader, who straightaway commanded his comrades to offer no injury to the strangers.
8Mary also saw the ray.
9The robber now took the Holy Family to his home, and told his wife how strangely his heart had been moved.
10The people were at first shy and shamefaced, something very unusual for them; still they approached, little by little, and gathered around the Holy Family, who had seated themselves in a corner on the ground.
11Some of the men went in and out, while the woman brought to Mary little rolls, fruits, honeycomb, and cups containing something to drink.
12The ass also was placed under shelter.
13The woman cleared out a small room for Mary and brought her a little tub of water in which to bathe the Child.
14She also dried the swathing bands for her at the fire.
15The husband was deeply impressed by the demeanor of the Holy Family, and especially the appearance of the Child.
16He said to his wife, “This Hebrew Child is no ordinary child.
17Beg the Lady to allow us to wash our leprous child in His bathing water.
18It may, perhaps, do it some good.”
19The wife went to request the favor of the Blessed Virgin; but before she had time to speak, Mary bade her take the water she had used for Jesus’ bath, wash the sick child in it, and it would become cleaner than it was before attacked by the disease.
20The boy was about three years old and stiff from leprosy.
21His mother carried him in and put him into the bath.
22Wherever the water touched him, the leprosy fell like scales to the bottom of the tub; the boy became clean and well.
23The mother was out of herself with joy; she wanted to embrace Mary and the Child Jesus.
24But Mary, stretching out her hand, warded her off; she would allow neither the Child nor herself to be touched by her.
25She told her to dig a hole deep down to a rock, and pour the water just used into it, that she might always have it for similar purposes.
26Mary spoke with her long, and exacted from her a promise to embrace the first opportunity of escape from her present abode.
27The people were all delighted; they stood around the Holy Family gazing at them in wonder.
28During the night, other members of their band came to the hut, and to them the boy’s cure was related.
29The robbers’ reverential bearing toward the Holy Family was so much the more remarkable, since I saw that night many travelers, attracted to their hut by the light, immediately taken prisoner and carried deep into the forest to an immense cave that served for their special storehouse.
30It lay under a thicket, the entrance closely concealed.
31In it were clothes, carpets, meat, goats, sheep, and innumerable other stolen things, all in profusion.
32I saw also boys about seven or eight years old whom the robbers had kidnapped.
33They were cared for by an old woman who lived in the cave.
34Mary slept none that night; she sat upon her couch on the floor perfectly still.
35At early dawn the Holy Family started again on their journey in spite of the robber and his wife, who wanted them to stay longer.
36They took with them a supply of provisions put up by their grateful host and hostess who also accompanied them a part of the way, that they might escape the snares.
37The robber and his wife took leave of the Holy Family with expressions of deep feeling, uttering these remarkable words: “Remember us wherever you go!” Upon hearing them, I had a vision in which I saw that the cured boy afterward turned out to be the Good Thief who on the cross said to Jesus: “Remember me when Thou comest into Thy Kingdom.”
38The robber’s wife, after some time, joined those that dwelt around the balsam garden.
39The Holy Family went from here further on into the desert.
40When they had again lost all trace of anything like a path, they were a second time surrounded by all kinds of animals, among them huge winged lizards and even serpents, which pointed out the way to them.
41At a later period, when unable to advance through the sandy plain in which they were, I saw a very lovely miracle.
42On either side of the road sprouted up the plant Rose of Jericho, with its crisped branches, its tiny flowers in the center, and its straight root.
43On they went now right joyously, watching as far as the eye could see these plants springing up, and so across the whole plain.
44I saw that it was revealed to the Blessed Virgin that, at some future day the people of the country would gather these roses and sell them to travelers in exchange for bread.
45The name of this region sounded like Gaza, or Goze.
46I saw the Holy Family arrive at a town and district called Lepe or Lape, in which were numerous canals and ditches with high dams.
47I saw them crossing the water on a raft.
48Mary sat on a log, and the ass was standing in something like a trough, or tub.
49Two ugly, brown-complexioned, half-naked men with flat noses and protruding lips, ferried them over.
50Our holy travelers came now to the house on the outskirts of the town; but the occupants were so rough and pitiless that, without saying a word, Mary and Joseph moved further on.
51I think this was the first pagan Egyptian city they had yet reached.
52They had made, up to this time, ten days’ journey in the Jewish country and then in the wilderness.
53I next saw the Holy Family on Egyptian territory, in a level, green country full of pasture grounds.
54In the trees were stationed idols like swathed dolls, or like fishes wrapped in broad bands upon which were figures or letters.
55Occasionally, I saw people fat, but short in stature, approaching these idols and venerating them.
56The Holy Family sought a little rest under the cattle shed, the cattle going out of their own accord to make room for them.
57They were in want of food, having neither bread nor water.
58Mary no longer had nourishment for her Child, and no one gave them anything.
59Every species of human misery was experienced by them during this flight.
60At last, some shepherds drew near to water their cattle.
61They, too, would have gone away without giving them anything, had not Joseph’s entreaties moved them to unlock the well and allow them to have a little water.
62Again, I saw the Holy Family weary and exhausted in a forest, at whose egress stood a slender date tree, the fruit all clustered on top.
63Mary approached the tree, the Child Jesus on her arm, prayed and raised the Child up to it.
64Instantly the tree bowed down its top as if kneeling, so that Mary could gather all its fruit.
65It afterward remained in that position.
66I saw Mary dividing a quantity of the fruit among the naked children who had run after them from the last village.
67At a quarter of an hour’s distance from this tree, stood another unusually large one of the same kind, very high, and hollow like an old oak.
68In it the Holy Family lay concealed from the people that followed them.
69That evening I saw them taking shelter within the walls of a ruined place, where they stayed overnight.


Chapter 2

The Balsam Garden

1On the next day, the Holy Family continued their journey through a sandy, desolate wilderness.
2Famishing for water and exhausted by weariness, they sat down on one of the sand hills, and the Blessed Virgin sent up a cry to God.
3Suddenly, a stream of pure water gushed forth at her side.
4Joseph removed the sand hill that was over it, and a clear, beautiful, little fountain jetted up.
5He made a channel for it, and it flowed over quite a large space, disappearing again near its source.
6Here they refreshed themselves, and Mary bathed the Child Jesus, while Joseph gave drink to the ass and filled the water bottles.
7I saw all kinds of animals like turtles drinking at the gushing waters.
8They did not appear at all afraid of the Holy Family.
9The soil over which the water had flowed soon began to clothe itself with verdure, and numbers of balsam trees afterward grew there.
10When the Holy Family returned from Egypt, those trees were large enough to furnish balsam for their refreshment.
11The place soon grew into a little settlement.
12Wherever the heathens planted these trees they withered.
13They thrived only when the Jews whom the Holy Family had known in this country went to live there.
14I think the wife of that robber whose boy had been cured of leprosy by the bath of the Child Jesus went there, too, for she soon escaped from the robbers.
15Her boy, however, remained with them some time longer.
16A balsam hedge surrounded the garden, and in its center were several large fruit trees.
17At a subsequent period, another large well was dug, out of which quantities of water were raised by means of a wheel turned by oxen.
18This water mingled with that of Mary’s spring and watered the whole garden; unmixed, it would have proved injurious.
19I have seen that the oxen employed in turning the wheel could not by any means be forced to work from Saturday noon till early on Monday morning.


Chapter 3

The Holy Family Reach Heliopolis

1I saw the Holy Family on their way to Heliopolis.
2From their last night lodgings they were accompanied thither by a good man who, I think, was one of the workmen on that canal over which they had been ferried.
3They now crossed a long and very high bridge over a wide river (the Nile), which appeared to have several branches, and came to a place before the city gate which was surrounded by a kind of promenade.
4Here on a tapering pedestal, stood a great idol with the head of an ox, and in its arms something like the figure of a swathed child.
5The idol was encom-passed by a circle of benches, or tables of stone upon which the worshippers laid their sacrifices.
6Not far off was a very large tree, under which the Holy Family sat down to rest.
7They had scarcely seated themselves when the earth began to quake, the idol tottered, and tilted over.
8A hue and cry instantly arose from the people, and many of the workmen on the canal in the neighborhood came rushing up.
9But the good man who had accompanied the Holy Family started with them for the city.
10They were already at the opposite side of the idol place when the terrified crowd, with menacing and abusive words, angrily surrounded them.
11Suddenly the earth heaved, the huge tree fell, its roots breaking up out of the ground, and there arose a lake of muddy water into which the idol splashed.
12It sank so deep that one could scarcely see its horns, and some of the most wicked of the bystanders sank with it.
13The Holy Family now entered the city unmolested, and put up near an idolatrous temple, a large stone building containing many rooms.
14Some of the idols in the temples of the city were likewise overturned.
15Heliopolis is also called On.
16Aseneth, wife of the Egyptian Joseph, resided here with the pagan priest Putiphar, and here also Dionysius the Areopagite studied.
17The city extends to a great distance around the many-branched river.
18One sees it from afar lying high above the general level.
19The river flows through it under the arches that support some of the buildings.
20Great logs lie in some parts of the river branches, placed there to enable the inhabitants to cross.
21I saw the ruins of enormous buildings, huge masses of heavy masonry, towers half standing, and even temples almost entire.
22I saw, too, pillars like towers, around the outside of which one could mount to the top.
23The Holy Family dwelt under a low colonnade, in which there were other dwellings besides their own.
24The supporting pillars were rather low, some round, some square, and above ran a highway for the accommodation of vehicles and pedestrians.
25Opposite this colonnade was a pagan temple with two courts.
26Joseph put up before their little abode a screen of light woodwork.
27There was room for the ass, also.
28The screen, or light wall that Joseph put up, was of the same kind as he was accustomed to make.
29I remarked behind a similar screen and set up against the wall, an altar consisting of a small table covered with red and over that a white, transparent cloth; on it stood a lamp.
30I saw St. Joseph working at home, and often also abroad.
31He made long rods with round knobs at the ends, little three-legged stools with a handle by which to grasp them, and a certain kind of basket.
32He made, also, a great many light, wicker partitions, and little, light towers, some hexagonal, others octagonal.
33They were formed of long, thin boards, tapering toward the top and ending in a knob.
34They had an entrance, and were large enough to allow a man to sit inside as in a sentry box; they had steps outside, up which one could mount.
35I saw little towers like these standing here and there before the pagan temples, also on the flat roofs of the houses.
36People used to sit in them; perhaps they were watch houses, or maybe they were intended as screens from the sun.
37I saw the Blessed Virgin weaving tapestry and doing another kind of work.
38For the latter she used a staff on the top of which a knot was fastened.
39I cannot say whether she was spinning or not.
40I often saw people visiting her and the little Infant Jesus.
41The Child lay on the ground by Mary’s side, in a kind of cradle like a little boat.
42Sometimes I saw it raised on a frame like a sawing-jack.
43There were not many Jews in Heliopolis, and I saw them going about with a downcast look as if they had no right to live there.
44North of Heliopolis, between it and the Nile, which there divides into several branches, lay the little territory of Goshen, and in it a little place cut up by canals, among which dwelt numbers of Jews whose religious ideas were very much confused.
45Several of them became acquainted with the Holy Family, and Mary did all kinds of feminine work for them, receiving as payment bread and other provisions.
46The Jews in the Land of Goshen had a temple, which they compared with the Temple of Solomon; but it was very different.
47Not far from his dwelling, Joseph built an oratory where the resident Jews, who possessed no such place of their own, used to assemble with the Holy Family for prayer.
48It was surmounted by a light cupola which could be thrown open, thus enabling the worshippers to stand under the open sky.
49In the center of the hall stood an altar, or table of sacrifice, covered, as usual, with red and white; on it lay rolls of parchment.
50The priest, or teacher, was a very old man.
51The men and women were not so separated from one another at prayer as in Palestine; the men stood on one side, the women on the other.
52The Holy Family dwelt a little more than a year at Heliopolis.
53They had much to suffer from the Egyptians who hated and persecuted them, on account of their overturned idols; and as the houses were all solidly built, Joseph could not find work at his trade.
54They left Heliopolis, therefore, but not before they had learned from an angel of the slaughter of the Bethlehemite babes.
55Both Mary and Joseph were deeply grieved, and the Child Jesus, who was now able to walk, being a year-and-a-half old, shed tears the whole day.


Chapter 4

The Murder of The Innocent Children

1I saw the mothers with their boys, from infants in the arms up to the age of two years, going to Jerusalem.
2They were from those different places around the Holy City, in which Herod had placed garrisons and in which, by means of officials, he had issued a proclamation to that effect; viz.
3from Bethlehem, Gilgal, and Hebron.
4I saw many women even from the Arabian frontiers taking their children to Jerusalem, and these had more than a day’s journey to make.
5The mothers went in bands, some with two children and riding on asses.
6On their arrival in the city, they were all conducted to a large building, and the husbands who accompanied some of them dismissed.
7The mandate was joyously obeyed, for the poor people imagined they were going to receive a reward.
8The building into which the mothers and their children were ushered, was not far from the house occupied by Pilate at a later period.
9It stood alone, and so encompassed by walls that no one outside could hear anything going on within.
10A gateway through double walls led into a large court enclosed on all sides by buildings.
11Those to the right and left were of one story; that in the middle, which looked like an old, deserted synagogue, was two stories in height.
12From all three, doors opened into the court.
13The middle building was a hall of justice, for I saw in the court before it a stone block, pillars with chains, and such trees as could be bound together by their branches and then suddenly snapped asunder, in order to tear people to pieces.
14The mothers were led through the court and into the two side buildings, where they were shut up.
15It looked to me at first as if they were in a sort of hospital, or lazar house.
16When they saw themselves thus unexpectedly deprived of liberty, they began to fear, to cry, and to lament.
17The lower story of the court of justice was a great hall like a prison, or guardroom; the upper one was also a large hall from which windows opened upon the court.
18The officers of justice were assembled in the latter, rolls of writing lying before them on tables.
19Herod himself was there.
20He wore his crown and a purple mantle bordered with black and lined with white fur.
21He stood at the windows with many others, looking down upon the slaughter of the Innocents.
22The mothers, one by one, with their boys, were summoned from the side buildings into the great hall under the judgment hall.
23On their entrance, the children were taken from them by the soldiers and carried out into the court where about twenty others were actively at work with swords and lances, piercing the little creatures through throat and heart.
24Some of the children were still in swaddling clothes, infants in the mother’s arms; while others, able to run around, wore little woven dresses.
25The soldiers did not remove the children’s clothing but, having pierced them through the heart and throat, they grasped them by one arm or leg and slung them together in a heap.
26It was a terrible sight! The mothers were, one after another, pushed back into the large hall by the soldiers.
27When the fate of their little ones dawned upon them, they raised a frightful cry, tore their hair, and clung to one another.
28There were so many of them and, toward the last, they were so crowded together that they could scarcely stir.
29I think the slaughter lasted till near evening.
30The bodies of the murdered children were buried together in a great pit in the court.
31I saw the mothers that night fettered, and taken back to their homes by the soldiers.
32Similar scenes were enacted in other places, for the massacre was carried on during several days.
33The number of the Holy Innocents was indicated to me by another number which sounded like and which I had to repeat until, I think, the whole amounted to seven hundred and seven, or seven hundred and seventeen.
34The place of the children’s massacre in Jerusalem was the subsequent hall of justice, and not far from that of Pilate; but it was at his time very greatly changed.
35At Christ’s death, I saw the pit in which the murdered children were buried, fall in.
36Their souls appeared and left the place.
37Elizabeth had fled with John into the desert.
38After a long search, she found a cave, and there she remained with him for forty days.
39After that, I saw that an Essenian belonging to the community on Mount Horeb and a relative of Anna the Prophetess, brought food to John, at first every eight, afterward every fourteen days, and otherwise provided for him.
40Before Herod’s persecution, John could have been hidden in the neighborhood of his parents’ house; but he had made his escape into the desert impelled by divine inspiration.
41He was destined to grow up in solitude, apart from intercourse with his fellow beings, and destitute of the customary nourishment of man.
42I saw that that wilderness produced certain fruits, berries, and herbs.


Chapter 5

The Holy Family Go to Matarea

1The Holy Family left Heliopolis on account of the persecution they there endured and because Joseph could not obtain work.
2They took byroads and went still further into the country, journeying southward toward Memphis.
3Passing through a little town not far from Heliopolis, they halted in the forecourt of an open, pagan temple, and sat down to rest; when, all on a sudden, down tumbled the idol and fell to pieces.
4It had the head of an ox with triple horns, and several cavities in the body to receive the sacrifices that were to be consumed.
5At once arose a tumult among the pagan priests; they seized the Holy Family and threatened them with punishment.
6But one of them represented to his companions, as they were consulting what measures to take, that the best thing for them to do would be to commend themselves to the God of these strangers; for he remembered, he said, what plagues had come upon their forefathers when they had persecuted those people, and that upon the night of their departure from Egypt the firstborn in every house had died.
7These words were effectual, and the Holy Family was left in peace.
8The pagan priest who had spoken for them went soon after to Matarea with several of his people, and there joined the Holy Family and the Jewish community.
9Mary and Joseph next went to Troja, a place on the eastern side of the Nile, opposite Memphis.
10It was large and very dirty.
11They had some idea of remaining there, but they were not well received; indeed, they could get not even a drink of water, much less a few dates for which they begged.
12Memphis lay west of the Nile, which was at that point very broad and contained some islands.
13A part of the city lay also on this side of the river and, in Pharao’s time, a large palace with gardens and high towers, from which Pharao’s daughter often looked out on the country around.
14I saw the spot upon which, among the tall bulrushes, the child Moses was found.
15Memphis was like three cities in one, for it was built on both sides of the Nile, and appeared also to be connected with Babylon, a city lying eastward of the river and nearer to its mouth.
16In Pharao’s time, the country in general around the Nile between Heliopolis, Babylon, and Memphis, was so covered with high stone dams and buildings, and so linked together by canals, that those three cities presented the appearance of one large city.
17But at the time of the Holy Family, all were separate, immense wastes intervening between them.
18The holy travelers proceeded northward from Troja along the river toward Babylon, a dirty, low lying city.
19Between the Nile and Babylon, they took the route by which they had come and returned a distance of about two hours.
20Buildings in ruins were scattered here and there along the whole road.
21After crossing a small branch of the river, or a canal, they reached Matarea, which was built upon a tongue of land jutting out into the Nile.
22The river bathed the city on two sides.
23It was, in general, a wretched enough place, built only of date-wood and solid mud covered with rushes.
24Joseph found plenty of work here.
25He built more substantial houses of wickerwork with galleries around them, to which the occupants could go for air and recreation.
26Here the Holy Family dwelt in a dark, vaulted cave that lay in a retired spot on the land side, not far from the gate by which they had entered.
27Joseph, as at Heliopolis, built a light screen before it.
28One of the idols in a little temple fell at their arrival and later all the others did the same.
29The people were in consternation, but one of the priests quieted them by recalling to their remembrance the plagues of Egypt.
30After some time, as a little community of Jews and converted pagans gathered around the Holy Family, the priests gave over to them the little temple whose idol had fallen at their coming, and Joseph turned it into a synagogue.
31Joseph was like the patriarch of the community.
32He taught them how to sing the Psalms correctly, for Judaism in those parts had greatly deteriorated.
33Only the poorest Jews dwelt here in Babylon, and that in the most wretched dens and caves.
34But in the Jewish settlement between On and the Nile, they were numerous and better off.
35They had a regular temple, for they had lapsed into frightful idolatry.
36They had a golden calf, a figure with an ox’s head, around which were ranged other representations of animals like polecats, or ferrets.
37These last mentioned animals defend people against the crocodile.
38They had, too, an imitation of the Ark of the Covenant and horrible things in it.
39The idolatry they practiced was of the most shameful kind and, in a subterranean hall, they carried on the most infamous wickedness, deluded by the hope that from it their messiah should come forth.
40They were exceedingly stiff-necked, and would not be converted.
41Later on, however, many of them left that settlement and went to Babylon, about two hours distant.
42In doing so, they could not, on account of the numerous dykes and canals, travel by a straight road; they had to make a detour around On.
43These Jews of the Land of Goshen had already made the acquaintance of the Holy Family, while the latter abode in On.
44Mary while there had done various kinds of work for them, such as knitting and embroidering covers and bands.
45She would never undertake works for vanity or extravagance, but only useful things and religious vestments.
46I saw women bringing work to her, which they wanted done in accordance with the requirements of vanity and fashion, and Mary returning it although so much in need of the pay she would have received for it.
47The women mocked and scornfully derided her.
48The Holy Family at first suffered greatly from want.
49Good water could not be had and wood failed; the inhabitants used only dried grass and reeds for their cooking.
50The Holy Family generally ate cold food.
51Joseph had plenty to do.
52He improved the poor huts for the people; but they treated him almost like a slave, giving him for his labor only what they themselves thought proper.
53Sometimes he brought home something as a remuneration for his work, and sometimes he brought nothing.
54The people were very unskillful in building their huts.
55They had no wood, excepting here and there a log or two; and even if they had had wood, they had no tools to shape it, for they had only knives of bone or stone.
56Joseph had brought the most necessary tools with him.
57The Holy Family were soon settled somewhat comfortably.
58They had little stools and tables, wicker screens, and a well-ordered fireplace also.
59The Egyptians ate sitting flat on the ground.
60In the wall of Mary’s sleeping place I saw a recess that Joseph had hollowed out, and in it was Jesus’ little bed.
61Mary’s couch was beside it, and I have often seen her by night kneeling in prayer to God before that little bed.
62Joseph slept in another enclosed corner.
63The oratory of the Holy Family was in a passage outside.
64Joseph and the Blessed Virgin had sepa-rate places in it and Jesus, too, had His little corner, where He prayed sitting, standing, or kneeling.
65There was a kind of little altar before the Blessed Virgin’s place, a small table covered with red and white.
66This table was like a leaf on hinges that could be let down from or put up against the wall.
67When let down, it disclosed a shelf in the wall itself and on the shelf were various objects, among them something that was held as sacred.
68I saw little bushes in pots formed like chalices; a withered, though still whole branch, on top of which was the lily that had blossomed in Joseph’s hand when he had been chosen by lot in the Temple for Mary’s spouse; and something like fine, thin, white sticks that were placed crosswise in the rounded part of the recess.
69The blossoming lily branch was the top of Joseph’s staff; it was stuck in a box about one-and-a-half inches in diameter.
70The little sticks that were arranged crosswise, were also in a box, a transparent one.
71There were about five of those little white sticks of the thickness of a coarse straw.
72They were crossed and bound in the middle to a kind of little sheaf.
73But one pays very little attention to such things when in vision; one’s thoughts are chiefly intent upon the holy personages there presented.
74I saw that the Holy Family had to subsist on fruits and bad water.
75They had been so long without good water that Joseph resolved to saddle the ass, take his leathern bottle, and start for the balsam spring in the desert in order to get some.
76But the Blessed Virgin was told in prayer by an angelic apparition that she should seek and find a spring at the back of their present abode.
77I saw her going over the hill in which they dwelt, to a deep vacant lot that lay at some distance between ruined walls.
78A large, old tree stood on that ground.
79Mary had in her hand a rod provided with a little scoop, such as the people of that country commonly carryon journeys.
80She stuck it into the ground near the tree, and a beautiful, clear stream of water instantly gushed forth.
81She hurried back joyfully to call Joseph, who soon removed the upper crust of earth and disclosed a well which had long ago been dug out and lined with masonry, but which for some time had been choked up and dry.
82He soon restored it and paved it around very beautifully with stones.
83At the side of the well toward which Mary had approached, lay a great stone almost like an altar.
84I think it was used for that purpose in former times.
85The Blessed Virgin after that often washed Jesus’ clothes and bands here, and dried them in the sun.
86The well remained unknown and was used only by the Holy Family until Jesus had grown large enough to go on little errands and even to bring water for His Mother.
87Once I saw Him taking other children to the well and giving them a drink of the water which He scooped up in a hollow, crooked leaf.
88The little ones told this to their parents, and so the well became known.
89Others now began to go to it, though it remained principally in the use of the Jews.
90Even in the time of the Holy Family, it possessed healing properties for the leprous.
91Later, when a little chapel had been built over the dwelling of the Holy Family, there was near the high altar a flight of steps leading down to their first abode.
92There I saw the spring.
93It was surrounded by dwellings, and its waters used for the cure of leprosy and similar diseases.
94Even the Turks kept a light burning in the little chapel, and dreaded being overtaken by some misfortune if they neglected it.
95But the last I saw of the spring, it was lying solitary, surrounded only by trees.
96I saw the Boy Jesus bringing water from the well for His Mother for the first time.
97Mary was in prayer when the Boy slipped to the well with a bottle, and brought it back full of water.
98Mary was unspeakably affected when she saw Him coming back with the water.
99She knelt down and implored Him never to do that again, for He might fall into the well.
100But Jesus replied that He would take care, and that He wanted to render her that service whenever she needed it.
101If Joseph happened to be working at a little distance from home, and did he leave a tool lying behind him, I used to see the Boy Jesus running after it and bringing it to him.
102The Boy noticed everything.
103I think the joy that Mary and Joseph experienced on His account, must have outweighed all their sufferings.
104Though perfectly childlike, He was very wise, skilled in everything; He knew and understood everything.
105I often saw Mary and Joseph filled with unspeakable admiration.
106When the Boy Jesus took to their owners the covers embroidered or woven by His Mother, who hoped to receive bread in return for her work, I often saw Him teased at first, and consequently sad.
107But after awhile, the Holy Family was very much loved by the people.
108I saw other children giving Jesus figs and dates, while many of their elders sought the Holy Family for help and consolation.
109All in trouble said, “Let us go to the Jewish Child.”
110 I saw the Boy going on all kinds of errands, even to a Jewish town a mile distant, to get bread in exchange for His Mother’s work.
111The wild animals, numerous on His route, did Him no harm; on the contrary, they and even the serpents showed Him affection.
112Once I saw Him going with other children to the Jewish town; He was weeping bitterly over the degradation of the Jews.
113When He went for the first time alone to that Jewish town, He wore, also for the first time, the brown robe woven by Mary.
114It was trimmed around the border with yellowish flowers.
115I saw Him kneeling and praying on the way.
116Two angels appeared to Him and spoke of Herod’s death, but He said nothing of it to His parents.


Chapter 6

The Return of the Holy Family from Egypt

1I saw the Holy Family’s departure from Egypt.
2Herod was long since dead, but danger still threat-ened and they could not return.
3I saw St. Joseph, who was always busy at his trade, very much troubled one evening.
4The people for whom he had been working had given him nothing; consequently, he had nothing to take home where there was so much need.
5He knelt down in the open air and prayed.
6He was greatly afflicted; his sojourn among these people was becoming intolerable.
7They practiced infamous idolatry, even sacrificing deformed children.
8The parent that sacrificed a healthy, well-formed child, was thought to be very pious.
9They had, besides, still more shameful rites that they carried on in secret.
10Even the Jews in the Jewish towns were to Joseph objects of horror.
11While in his trouble he prayed to God for help, I saw an angel appear to him.
12He bade him arise, and on the following morning depart from Egypt by the public high road.
13He told him also not to fear, for that he would accompany him.
14I saw Joseph hastening with the news to the Blessed Virgin and Jesus, and all setting to work to get their few movables packed together on the ass.
15Next morning their intention to depart having become known, crowds of sorrowing neighbors came to them, bringing with them all kinds of gifts in little vessels of bark.
16Several mothers brought their children.
17There was among them a noble lady with a little boy of several years.
18She called him Mary’s son, because having long abandoned the hope of having a boy, this child had been vouchsafed to her at Mary’s prayer.
19She gave to the Boy Jesus triangular coins, yellow, white, and brown.
20Jesus first looked at them and then at His Mother.
21This lady’s little son was later on admitted by Jesus into the number of His disciples, and was named Deodatus.
22The mother’s name was Mira.
23The people of the place, of whom there were more pagans than Jews, were sincerely grieved at the Holy Family’s departure, though a few were glad.
24These last looked upon them as sorcerers who obtained all they desired through the help of Lucifer, the prince of devils.
25The Jews could no longer be recognized as Jews, so deeply were they sunk in idolatry.
26The Holy Family started, accompanied by all their friends.
27They took the direction between On and the Jewish town, turning away from On a little to the south, in order to reach the balsam garden.
28They wanted to rest there awhile and replenish their water supply.
29The garden was already flourishing.
30The balsam trees were as tall as moderately large grapevines and in four rows surrounded the garden, which had an entrance.
31There were sycamores and all kinds of fruit trees, some like dates.
32The spring sent a stream around the whole garden.
33The friends that had accompanied them here took leave, but the Holy Family remained for some hours.
34Joseph had made some little vessels out of bark; they were covered with pitch, very smooth and nice.
35He snapped from the reddish balsam twigs the clover-like leaves, and hung the flasks underneath, in order to gather the balsam drops for the journey.
36When they stopped to rest, he often made, for their ordinary use, vessels and flasks of that kind out of bark.
37The Blessed Virgin washed and dried some things here.
38After having rested and refreshed themselves, they proceeded on their way by the common high road.
39I had many visions of their journey, which was made without any special danger to them.
40Mary was often very much distressed, because walking through the hot sand was so painful for the Boy Jesus.
41Joseph had made for Him, out of bark, shoes that reached above the ankle where they were firmly fastened; still I saw the holy travelers frequently pausing while Mary shook the sand out of the Child’s shoes.
42She herself wore only sandals.
43Jesus was dressed in His little brown robe, and they often had to seat Him on the ass.
44For protection against the scorching rays of the sun, all three wore very broad hats made of bark and fastened under the chin with a string.
45I saw them passing by many cities, but I now recall only the name Ramses.
46At last, I saw them in Gaza, where they stopped for three months.
47There were many pagans in that city.
48Joseph did not want to return to Nazareth, but to go to Bethlehem; still he was undecided, because he heard that Archelaus was now reigning over Judea, and he, too, was very cruel.
49But an angel appeared and put an end to his doubts by telling him that he should return to Nazareth.
50Anne was still living.
51She and some of her relatives were the only ones that knew where the Holy Family were during all those years.
52I had a glimpse of the Boy Jesus, now seven years old, as He walked between Mary and Joseph on their journey back to Judea from Egypt.
53I did not see the ass with them then, and they were carrying their bundles themselves.
54Joseph was about thirty years older than Mary.
55I saw them on a road in the desert, about two hours’ distance from John’s cave.
56The Boy Jesus, as He walked, gazed in that direction, and I saw that His soul was turning to John.
57At the same time, I saw John at prayer in his cave.
58An angel in the form of a boy appeared to him, telling him that the Saviour was passing by.
59John ran out of the cave and, with outstretched arms, flew toward the point that his Saviour was passing.
60He hopped about and danced with joy.
61This vision was most touching.
62John’s cave lay deeply buried in a hill.
63It was not much wider than his own little bed, though it extended some distance in length.
64The entrance was only a little opening, through which he used to swing himself out.
65In the top was an oblique aperture that admitted light.
66I saw in it a reed stand, upon which lay some honeycomb and dried locusts.
67The latter were yellow and speckled, as large, perhaps, as crabs.
68The desert in which Jesus fasted is four hours’ distance from here.
69John was clothed in his camel’s skin.
70The angel that appeared to him was like a boy of his own age.
71I saw him at different periods, small at first and then larger, just as if he were growing up with John.
72He was not always with him; he used to appear and disappear.


Chapter 7

John as a Child Growing Up In the Desert

1John had already been long in the desert before the Holy Family’s return from Egypt.
2That he had retired there at so young an age was due principally to divine inspiration and partly to his own inclinations, for he was of a meditative nature and loved solitude.
3He was never in a school; the Holy Ghost Himself taught him in the desert.
4He was much talked of even from his childhood, for the wonders attendant on his birth were known and a light was often seen around the child.
5Herod soon laid snares for him, and even before the children’s massacre, Elizabeth was obliged to flee with him into the desert.
6He could walk and help himself at the time.
7He took refuge not far from the first cave of Magdalen, and Elizabeth visited him sometimes.
8When in his sixth or seventh year, I saw him again led into the desert by his mother.
9When Eliz-abeth left the house with the boy, Zachary was not home.
10He loved John so much and his grief at losing him was so great that he was obliged to absent himself in order not to witness his departure.
11He had, however, given him his blessing; for he was in the habit of blessing both mother and child whenever he left home.
12John wore a garment of skin.
13It passed from left to right over the shoulder and breast, was fastened under the right arm, and hung down behind.
14This was his only garment.
15His hair was brownish and darker than that of Jesus.
16He bore in his hand a white staff which he had brought with him from home, and which he always kept in the desert.
17I saw him as just described hastening across the country by the hand of his mother.
18Elizabeth was a tall, active, old woman with a small, delicate face, and she was completely enveloped in a large mantle.
19John often ran on before her, hopping and jumping, perfectly unrestrained and childlike in action, though not distracted in soul.
20I saw them crossing a river.
21There was no bridge at that point, and so they crossed on a raft that was floating on the water.
22Elizabeth was a very resolute person, no difficulty daunted her; she herself rowed the raft across, using for that purpose the branch of a tree.
23They now turned eastward and entered a ravine, rocky and desolate above, but lower down covered with bushes and overgrown with strawberries.
24John now and then ate one.
25After going some distance into the ravine, Elizabeth took leave of John.
26She blessed him, pressed him to her heart, kissed him on the cheeks and forehead, and turned away, looking back at him as she retraced her steps, weeping.
27But the boy appeared wholly unconcerned, and quietly walked on deeper into the ravine.
28I followed the child with a feeling of uneasiness at his going so far from his mother, and fearing that he would not be able to find his way home again.
29But just then, a voice said to me, “Be not uneasy.
30The child knows well what he is about.”
31 I went with him and, in several visions, saw his whole after life in the desert.
32He often told me himself how he denied himself in every way and mortified his senses, his understanding becoming clearer and clearer, learning in an unexplainable way something from everything around him.
33I saw him when a child playing with flowers and animals.
34The birds were particularly familiar with him.
35They lighted upon his head when he was walking or praying, and perched upon his staff when he laid it across the branches.
36There they sat in numbers, while he watched them and played with them.
37I saw him also going after other animals, following them into their dens, feeding them, playing with them, or earnestly watching them.
38At the opposite extremity of this rocky ravine, the country was somewhat more open, and John pressed on until he reached a little lake with a low shore covered with white sand.
39I saw him there wading far out into the water.
40The fish swam up and gathered around him; he seemed quite at home with them.
41He lived in this region a long time, and I saw that he wove for himself out of branches a sleeping hut among the bushes.
42It was very low and only large enough to allow him to lie in it.
43Both here and afterward in other places, I often saw by him radiant figures, angels, with whom he treated fearlessly and confidently, though most reverently.
44They appeared to be teaching him, directing his attention to different things.
45He had fastened a piece of wood to his staff, thus giving it the form of a cross, also a strip of broad grass, or bark, or leaves like a little flag.
46He often played with it, waving it here and there.
47While he lived in this part of the desert, I saw his mother visiting him twice, but they did not meet at this spot.
48He must have known when she was coming, for he always went some distance to meet her.
49Elizabeth brought him a tablet with a slender reed for writing.
50After his father’s death, John went secretly to Juttah, to console Elizabeth.
51He remained concealed with her for some time.
52She told him many things of Jesus and the Holy Family, some of which he noted down with strokes on his tablet.
53Elizabeth wanted him to go with her to Nazareth, but he would not.
54He returned again to the desert.
55Once when Zachary had gone with a herd to the Temple, he was set upon by Herod’s soldiers and rudely maltreated in a narrow pass on the side of Jerusalem nearest to Bethlehem, at a spot whence the city could not be seen.
56The soldiers dragged him into a prison on that side of Mount Zion by which, at a later period, the disciples used to ascend.
57Zachary was frightfully maltreated, tortured, and at last pierced with a sword, because he would not disclose John’s retreat.
58Elizabeth was at the time in the desert with John.
59When she returned to Juttah, he accompanied her part of the way, and then went back to the desert.
60On reaching Juttah, Elizabeth learned of the murder of her husband and great were her lamentations.
61Zachary was buried by his friends in the vicinity of the Temple.
62He is not that Zachary who was slain between the altar and the Temple and whom I saw at the time of the Crucifixion with the other risen dead.
63He issued from that part of the wall in which the aged Simeon once had his cell for prayer, and walked about the Temple.
64The last Zachary was murdered in a struggle that had taken place among many at the Temple, concerning the genealogy of the Messiah and certain privileges and places of individual families.
65Elizabeth’s sorrow was so great that she could no longer bear to remain in Juttah, without John; consequently, she returned to him in the desert.
66She soon after died there and was buried by an Essenian, a relative of Anna the Prophetess.
67The house in Juttah, a very handsomely ordered one, was occupied by her sister’s daughter.
68John secretly returned to it once after his mother’s death, after which he buried himself still deeper in the desert and thenceforth was altogether alone.
69I saw him journeying to the south around the Dead Sea, then up the eastern side of the Jordan, from wilderness to wilderness toward Kedar and even toward Gessur.
70When he passed from one wilderness to another, I saw him running through broad fields by night.
71He went to that region where long after I saw John the Evangelist sitting and writing under the high trees.
72Under those trees grew bushes with berries, of which he sometimes ate.
73I saw him also eating a certain herb that bears a white flower and has five round leaves like clover.
74We have at home herbs like them, only smaller.
75They grow under the hedges, and the leaves have a sourish taste.
76When I was a child I used to love to chew them while minding the cattle off in the solitary fields, because I had seen John eating them.
77I also saw him drawing forth from holes in the trees and picking out of moss on the ground lumps of some brownish-looking stuff, which he ate.
78I think it was wild honey, for it was very plentiful there.
79The skin that he had brought with him from home, he now wore around his loins, and over his shoulders hung a brown, shaggy cover which he had woven himself.
80There were in the desert wool-bearing animals which ran tamely around John, and camels with long hair on their neck.
81They stood most patiently and allowed him to pull it out.
82I saw him twisting the hair into cords and weaving from them that covering which he wore hanging around him when he appeared among men and baptized.
83I saw him in continual and familiar communication with angels, by whom he was instructed.
84He slept upon the hard rock and under the open sky, ran over rough stones through thorns and briers, disciplined himself with thistles, wore himself out working on trees and stones, and lay prostrate in prayer and contemplation.
85He leveled roads, made little bridges, and changed the course of well springs.
86I often saw him writing in the sand with a reed, kneeling and standing motionless in ecstasy, or praying with outstretched arms.
87His penance and mortification became more and more severe, his prayer longer and more fervent.
88He saw the Saviour only three times face to face with his bodily eyes.
89But Jesus was with him in spirit; and John, who was constantly in the prophetic state, saw in spirit the actions of Jesus.
90I saw John when full grown.
91He was a powerful, earnest man.
92He was standing by a dry well in the desert, and appeared to be in prayer.
93A light hovered over him like a cloud, and it seemed to me as if it came from on high, from the water above the earth.
94Then a light, shining stream fell over him into the basin below.
95While gazing on this torrent, I saw John no longer at the edge of the basin; he was in it, the shining water flowing over him, and the basin filled by the sparkling stream.
96Then again, I saw him, as at first, standing on the basin’s edge; but I did not see him out of it, nor coming out.
97I think that the whole was perhaps a vision which John himself had had, and by which he was instructed to begin to baptize; or it may have been a spiritual baptism bestowed upon him in vision.


Chapter 8

Feast Picture of John the Baptist

1I saw in the desert in which John dwelt a spiritual church rising up out of the waters that flowed in streams from on high, from Paradise, that floated in clouds, and welled up in fountains.
2The church was immeasurably vast; it seemed to be symbolical of Baptism, and it grew with the baptized.
3It was perfectly transparent like crystal.
4An octagonal tower arose from the interior and reached up far out of sight.
5Under it was a great fountain like the baptism fountain of John which he had formed in the desert after a model shown him in vision.
6In the tower grew a genealogical tree upon which appeared John and his ancestors.
7There was also an altar, and a wonderful representation of John’s conception, birth, circumcision, and life in the desert, of the baptism of Jesus and John’s beheading.
8Far up in the tower, as if on a ladder reaching to Heaven, were seen in admirable order the whole host of Saints, the entire history of the Promise and the Redemption, and the abodes of the Blessed, endless in number.
9High above all the rest hovered the Blessed Virgin in a mantle so wide as to cover all.
10All these representations were white and transparent.
11And now came immense crowds from all sides, kings and peoples in all kinds of costumes; they looked like nations that were migrating.
12Many passed by the baptism church and went into the desert, where there is no water of life.
13Many others entered the church and knelt down by the baptism fountain, by the side of which stood John under the appearance that he presented as a child in the desert.
14He struck the water with his little staff and sprinkled it over them.
15And, no matter how tall they were on entering the church, all that were thus sprinkled became small.
16But many only passed in and out of the church.
17They who had become little ones, like unto those that enter the Heavenly Kingdom, ascended the high, wonderful tower on the ladder that reached to Heaven.
18There were at the baptism holy godparents.
19The whole church, which appeared to be a building and still was formed of water, floated on high as if supported by a cord let down from Heaven.


Chapter 9

The Holy Family at Nazareth. Jesus at the Age of Twelve in The Temple of Jerusalem

1There were three separate rooms in the house at Nazareth, that of the Mother of God being the largest and most pleasant; in it Jesus, Mary, and Joseph met to pray.
2I very seldom saw them together at other times.
3They stood at prayer, their hands crossed upon their breast, and they appeared to speak aloud.
4I often saw them praying by a light.
5They stood under a lamp that had several wicks, or near a kind of branched candlestick fastened to the wall, and upon which the flame burned.
6They were most of the time alone in their respective rooms, Joseph working in his.
7I saw him cutting sticks and laths, planing wood, and carrying up a beam, Jesus helping him.
8Mary was generally engaged sewing or knitting with little needles, at which she sat on the ground, her feet crossed under her, and a little basket at her side.
9They slept alone, each in a separate room.
0The bed consisted of a cover which in the morning was rolled up.
1I saw Jesus assisting His parents in every possible way, and also on the street and wherever oppor-tunity offered, cheerfully, eagerly, and obligingly helping everyone.
2He assisted His foster-father in his trade, or devoted Himself to prayer and contemplation.
3He was a model for all the children of Nazareth; they loved Him and feared to displease Him.

5And Jesus took it playfully and like a little child.
6He would beg the children affectionately to do so and so, would pray with them to His Heavenly Father for strength to become better, and would persuade them to acknowledge their faults and ask pardon on the spot.

0There lived in Nazareth an Essenian family related to Joachim.
1They had four sons, a few years older or younger than Jesus, named respectively, Cleophas, James, Judas, and Japhet.

7He was married and dwelt at Emmaus.

9Jesus was tall and slender with a delicate face and a beaming countenance and though pale, He was healthy-looking.
0His perfectly straight, golden hair was parted over His high, open forehead and fell upon His shoulders.
1He wore a long, light-brownish gray tunic, which reached to His feet, the sleeves rather wide around the hand.

Chapter 10

Death of St. Joseph. Jesus and Mary in Capharnaum

9After Joseph’s death, Jesus and Mary removed to a little village of only a few houses between Capharnaum and Bethsaida.
0A man named Levi, who was very much attached to the Holy Family, had given Jesus a house there in which to dwell.
1It stood alone surrounded by a ditch of standing water.
2A couple of Levi’s people also were in the house in the capacity of servants, and Levi himself supplied all necessaries from Capharnaum.
3It was to this little place that Peter’s father retired when he gave over to him the fishery at Bethsaida.
4Jesus had already many followers among the young people of Nazareth, but they were not faithful to Him.
5He walked with them in the country around the lake and went up to Jerusalem with them for the feasts.
6The Lazarus family in Bethania were already acquainted with the Holy Family.
7The Pharisees of Nazareth were against Jesus; they called Him a vagrant.
8Levi gave Him that house that He might, without fear of disturbance, live in it and gather His followers around Him.
9There was on the lake around Capharnaum, a region of extraordinarily fertile and charming valleys.
0There were several harvests during the year, and uncommonly beautiful leaves, blossoms, and fruits—all at the same time.
1Many distinguished Jews had gardens and castles there, Herod among the number.
2The Jews of Jesus’ time were no longer like their fathers; through commerce and their intercourse with heathens, they had become very corrupt.
3One never saw the women in public nor at work in the fields, excepting the very poorest gleaning some ears of corn.
4They were to be seen only on pilgrimages to Jerusalem and other holy places.
5Husbandry and all kinds of traffic were carried on mostly through slaves.
6I have seen all the cities of Galilee.
7Where now scarcely three villages are in existence, there were then almost a hundred and an innumerable crowd of people.
8Mary Cleophas, who with her third husband, the father of Simeon of Jerusalem, dwelt in Anne’s house near Nazareth, afterward removed with her boy Simeon to Mary’s in Nazareth.
9The rest of her family and her servants remained at Anne’s.
0When Jesus, a short time after, went from Capharnaum by way of Nazareth to the region of Hebron, He was accompanied by Mary as far as Nazareth, where she awaited His return.
She was always so solicitous about Him.
There came also to comfort the Holy Family on the death of St.
Joseph and to see Jesus again, Joses Barsabas, the son of Mary Cleophas by her second marriage with Sabas, and the three sons of her first marriage with Alpheus: Simon, James the Less, and Thaddeus, all three of whom already carried on business away from home.
They had had no close communication with Jesus since His childhood.
They knew in general of Simeon’s and Anne’s prophecies on the occasion of His Presentation in the Temple, but they attached no importance to them.
They preferred to follow John the Baptist, who soon after passed through these parts.

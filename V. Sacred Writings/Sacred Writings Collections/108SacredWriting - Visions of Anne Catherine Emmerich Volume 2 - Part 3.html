Sacred Writing

"Every writings must be evaluated based on the truths they contain."

From the Nativity of the Virgin Mary to the death of patriarch Saint Joseph

Chapter 1

Mary’s Entrance into the Temple and her Offering

1Zachary and the other men had already gone to the Temple, and now Mary was led thither by the women and the virgins.
2Anne and her elder daughter Mary Heli, with the little daughter of the latter, Mary Cleophas, walked first; then came Mary in her second suit, the sky-blue dress and mantle, her neck and arms adorned with garlands, and the flower wreathed candlestick in her hand.
3On either side walked three little maidens with similarly trimmed candlesticks.
4They were dressed in white embroidered with gold, and wore bluish mantles.
5They were quite covered with garlands, even their arms were twined with flowers.
6Then followed the other virgins and little girls, about twenty in number, all dressed beautifully, but somewhat differently though all wore mantles.
7Then came the elderly females.
8They could not proceed straight to the Temple from this point; they had to take a circuitous route of nearly half an hour.
9They passed through some streets and before Veronica’s house.
10From many of the dwellings the procession was saluted, the spectators gazing in wonder at the child and her beautiful train of attendants.
11There was something very extraordinary in Mary’s appearance.
12At the Temple, men were busy opening a large and wonderfully beautiful gate upon which were carved grapevines, ears of wheat, and heads of various kinds.
13It was the Golden Gate.
14The priests led the Holy Virgin up numerous steps to this gate.
15Joachim and Zachary met them at the gate, which opened into a long archway, and led them through several passages into a hall.
16Here Mary was again questioned by the priests, after which she was clothed in the third holiday suit, the violet-blue, embroidered one.
17And now Joachim went with the priests to offer sacrifice.
18He took fire from a certain place and stood between two priests at the altar.
19The approach to the altar from three sides was free, but not so on the fourth.
20At the four corners of the altar, stood small copper pillars and a pipe of the same metal, shaped like a large inverted funnel, which ended in a spiral tube.
21By this arrangement the smoke from the burning sacrifice rose and escaped over the head of the priest.
22On three sides of the altar a shelf could be drawn out to receive what was to be laid on the middle of it, since to reach that far would be impossible.
23When the sacrifice was kindled, Mary went with the women and children to her place of prayer in the women’s porch, where she and her young companions stood in the front row.
24The porch was separated from the court of the altar of burnt offerings by a wall, in which was a gate with a grating above.
25Through this gate Joachim entered the subterranean passage when, upon the day of Mary’s Immaculate Conception, he met Anne under the Golden Gate.
26The women back in the court could see the altar better, when mounted on steps raised in tiers.
27In another court was standing a crowd of white-robed boys belonging to the Temple, playing upon flutes and harps.
28After the sacrifice, a portable altar was set up under the arched gateway, and before it were placed a couple of steps.
29Zachary and Joachim, with some priests and two Levites, entered from the court of the altar of burnt offerings, carrying rolls and writing materials, while Anne led Mary to the steps before the altar.
30Mary knelt upon the steps, while Joachim and Anne, laying their hands on her head, uttered some words bearing reference to the offering of their child, which words were written down by the two Levites.
31Then one of the priests cut a lock of hair from the child’s head, and cast it upon a pan of live coals, after which he threw around her a brown veil.
32During this ceremony, the girls sang 44, the priests, 49, and the boys played on their musical instruments.
33And now the priests led the Holy Virgin up a long flight of steps in the wall that separated the sanctuary from the rest of the Temple.
34They stood her in something like a niche, from which she could see into the Temple where were ranged numbers of men who seemed to be consecrated to its service.
35Two priests stood at Mary’s side, and several others on the steps praying and reading aloud from rolls.
36Behind Mary and on the other side of the wall, a priest was standing at the altar of incense, only half of his person visible from the point at which Mary and her attendants were placed.
37Through an opening contrived for the purpose, one could cast incense upon the altar without entering the court.
38The priest now at the incense altar was a holy old man.
39While he offered sacrifice and the cloud of incense arose around Mary, I saw a vision, which grew in magnitude until at last it filled the whole Temple and obscured it.
40I saw above the heart of Mary the glory and the Mystery of the Ark of the Covenant.
41At first it looked exactly like the Ark of the Covenant; and lastly like the Temple itself.
42Out of the Mystery and before Mary’s breast, arose a chalice similar to that of the Last Supper; above it and just in front of her mouth appeared bread marked with a cross.
43Beams of light radiated around her, and in them shone her various types and symbols.
44The mysterious pictures of the Litany of Loretto and the other names and titles of Mary, I saw ranged up the whole flight of steps and around her.
45From her shoulders, right and left, stretched an olive and a cedar branch crosswise above an elegant palm tree with a small tuft of leaves that stood directly behind her.
46In the intervening spaces of this verdant cross, appeared all the instruments of Christ’s Passion.
47Over the vision hovered the Holy Spirit, a figure winged with glory, in appearance more human than dovelike.
48The heavens opened above Mary and the central point of the Heavenly Jerusalem, the City of God, floated over her with all the gardens, the palaces, and the dwellings of the future saints.
49Angels in myriads hovered around, and the glory that encircled her was full of angelic faces.
50Ah, who can express it! Infinite variety, unceasing change, all these pictures following quickly upon and, as it were, growing out of one another.
51Innumerable points of this vision, I have forgotten.
52All the splendor and magnificence of the Temple, the richly ornamented wall before which Mary was standing—all grew dark and somber.
53The whole Temple disappeared, for Mary and her glory alone was visible.
54In this vision, symbolical of Mary’s spiritual signification, I saw her not as a child, but full-grown.
55She hovered in the air. And through and through the vision, I still saw the priests, the incense offering, and everything else.
56Then the priest at the altar appeared to prophesy, and to call upon the people to thank God and to pray, for that great things were to come upon the child.
57The crowd in the Temple, greatly awed—although they had not seen the vision that I saw—maintained a solemn stillness.
58The vision faded away just as gradually as it had unfolded.
59At last, the Mystery of the Ark of the Covenant shone again in its glory over her heart, and the child once more stood there alone in her rich attire.
60Then the priests, among whom Zachary was one of those standing on the lower steps, led Mary down by the hand.
61One of them took the light from her and the little garlands off her arms, and handed them to the other girls. Mary was then led through a door into another hall where six other Temple virgins, their mistress Noemi, (who was the sister of Lazarus’s mother) Anna, and another female met them and scattered flowers before her.
62To them the priest delivered the child.
63When the singing was ended, Mary look leave of her parents.
64Joachim was especially affected.
65He took the little child up in his arms, pressed her to his heart, and said weeping: “Remember my soul before God.”
66Mary now accompanied the women and children belonging to the Temple to their dwelling on the north side, from which passages and winding stairs led up to little chambers adjoining the sanctuary and the Holy of Holies, where they went to pray.
67The others (that is, Mary’s relatives and friends) returned to the apartments near the entrance, and took a repast with the priests, the women apart.
68There were still in the Temple some devout adorers. Many had followed the procession to the entrance.
69There were numbers among those present who knew that Mary was a child of promise in her family.
70I remember, though not distinctly, that Anne had dropped some such expressions to her friends as: “Now does the vessel of the Promise enter the Temple.
71Now is the Ark of the Covenant in the Temple.”
72It was by a special manifestation of the Divine Will that this feast was so solemnly and magnificently celebrated.
73Joachim and Anne were indeed wealthy, but they lived very frugally.
74They gave all to the Temple and to the poor.
75I do not now remember how long it was that Anne took for herself nothing but cold victuals, but she treated her domestics generously and provided them with dowries.
76I think she and Joachim returned that same day with their whole company to Bethoron.
77I saw also a feast among the Temple children.
78They had a meal at which Mary had to question first the mistresses and then the maidens separately as to whether they were willing to have her among them.
79This was the custom.
80Then the girls had a dance among themselves. They stood two and two opposite one another and danced, changed places across, and formed figures in and out.
81There was no leaping, but certain swaying movements of the whole person, which seemed somewhat expressive of the Jewish character.
82Some of the girls accompanied the dance with the music of flutes, triangles, chimes, and an instrument that gave forth sounds at once strange and agreeable.
83It consisted of a little box with oblique sides, over which were stretched strings which the players touched with their fingers.
84The center of the box contained bellows out of which projected several pipes, some crooked, others straight.
85The performer pressed sometimes here, sometimes there on the center of the bellows which mingled its sounds with those of the strings.
86The instrument was rested either upon the knee of the performer, or upon a stool under which the knee was placed.
87In the evening, Noemi took Mary to her cell, from which she could see down into the Temple.
88Here Mary mentioned to Noemi her desire to get up more frequently in the night to pray, but Noemi refused her request for the present.
89The women belonging to the Temple wore white robes, long and wide, girdled at the waist.
90Their flowing sleeves were turned up when at work.
91Far back in the Temple were numerous chambers built in the wall and connected with the dwellings of the women.
92Mary’s cell was one of the most distant, one nearest the Holy of Holies.
93From the passage that led to it, one raised a curtain and stepped into an apartment, a sort of antechamber separated from the cell by a light, semicircular, movable screen.
94Here in the corners right and left, were shelves for clothing and other things.
95Opposite the door in the screen that led into the cell was an opening hung with gauze and tapestry, and looking down into the Temple.
96It was rather high in the wall; one had to mount upon steps to reach it.
97On the left of the cell, lay a cover rolled into a bundle, which Mary unrolled at night for a couch.
98A branched lamp stood in a niche of the wall.
99I saw the holy child standing on a stool near it and praying out of a roll with red knobs on the rod.
100It was indeed a touching sight. The child wore a little coarsely woven, striped dress, blue and white, with yellow flowers.
101A small round table like a stool stood in the room, and on it I saw Anna setting a dish of fruit the size of beans, and a little jug.
102The child was skillful far beyond her years.
103She could already work on little white cloths for the service of the Temple.
104The wall of her cell was inlaid with colored, triangular stones.
105I often saw the child Mary seized with holy longing for the Messiah and saying to Anna: “Oh, will the promised Child be born soon? Oh, if I could only see that Child! Oh, if only I am living when He is born!” Then Anna would give this reply: “Think how old I am and how long I have waited for that Child! And you—you are still so young!” And Mary would shed tears of longing for the promised Saviour.
106The maidens reared in the Temple under the care of the matrons occupied themselves with embroidery, with all kinds of ornamental work, and with cleansing the priestly garments and the vessels belonging to the Temple.
107From their cells, they could see into the Temple, pray and meditate.
108They were, by the fact of their parents’ having placed them there, entirely dedicated to the Lord.
109Upon reaching a certain age, they were given in marriage, for there was among the more enlightened Israelites the pious, though secret hope that from such a virgin dedicated to God, the Messiah would be born.
110I never saw that Herod built the Temple anew.
111Under him there were indeed many changes made in it; but at the time of Mary’s entrance, eleven years before the Birth of Christ, the Temple itself had not been touched.
112The additions and changes had been made as heretofore on the outbuildings alone.


Chapter 2

A Glance at the Obduracy of the Pharisees

1How obdurate and obstinate the priests and the Pharisees of the Temple were, may be discovered from the small esteem in which they held the distinctions bestowed upon the Holy Family.
2First Joachim’s offering was rejected; but after some months both his own and his wife’s were, by God’s command, received.
3Joachim was admitted even into the presence of the Holy of Holies and he, as well as Anne, was—though unknown to each other—led into the passage under the Temple.
4There they met, Mary was conceived, and priests awaited them at the entrance of this cave under the Temple—all that took place by God’s command.
5I have seen that sometimes, though not often, the sterile were commanded to be led in there.
6Mary entered the Temple in her fourth year, and in all things was she distinguished and remarkable.
7The sister of Lazarus’s mother was her teacher and nurse.
8Her whole manner of acting was so remarkable, so marvelous, that I have seen great rolls written by aged priests about her.
9I think they still lie hidden with other writings.
10Then came the wonderful manifestations at Joseph’s espousals and the blossoming of his rod, the accounts of the Three Kings and of the shepherds, the Presentation of Jesus, Anna’s and Simeon’s testimony, and the teaching of Jesus at the age of twelve in the Temple.
11But all this, the priests and Pharisees noticed not.
12Their mind was preoccupied by business and court affairs.
13Because the Holy Family lived in voluntary retirement and poverty, they were forgotten in the crowd.
14The more enlightened, however, such as Simeon, Anna, and others, knew of them.
15But when Jesus appeared and John bore witness to Him, the teaching of the Pharisees was so directly contradictory that, even if the signs of His coming had not been forgotten by them, they would certainly not have made them known.
16Herod’s reign and the Roman yoke had so involved them in quarrels and intrigues that their taste for spiritual things was weakened.
17They did not esteem John’s testimony, and they soon forgot him after he was beheaded.
18They cared little for the teaching and miracles of Jesus, and their ideas of the Prophets and the Messiah were altogether erroneous.
19It is not surprising, therefore, that they so shamefully treated Jesus, and put Him to death, that they disavowed His Resurrection, the wonderful signs that followed it, and even the fulfillment of His prophecy respecting the destruction of Jerusalem.
20Nor is it to be wondered at that they neglected the signs that heralded His advent, since He had not at that time either taught or wrought miracles.
21Were the blindness, the obduracy of these men not so incomprehensibly great, could it have lasted even to this day? 22When I go over the Way of the Cross in Jerusalem of the present day, I frequently see under a certain ruined building a large vault, or many adjoining vaults, which are partly fallen in and filled with water.
23Standing in the midst of the water, which rises almost to a level with it, is a table.
24From the center of the table to the roof of the vault, rises a pillar around which are hung little coffers filled with rolls of writings.
25Under the table also I saw rolls lying in the water.
26Perhaps these vaults were once burial places.
27They lie under Mount Calvary.
28I think the ruined building is the house wherein Pilate once dwelt, and the treasure will after some time be discovered.


Chapter 3

John Promised to Zachary

1I saw Zachary conversing with Elizabeth.
2He was telling her how sad he was because his turn to offer sacrifice in the Temple was drawing near, and how he dreaded the contempt that would there await him on account of his being childless.
3Zachary went twice a year to the Temple.
4He did not live at Hebron itself, but at a place called Juta about fifteen minutes’ walk from Hebron.
5The ruins of former buildings still lay between the two places, leading one to fancy that they had once been connected.
6Many such ruins were to be found on the other side of Hebron, for the place was once as large as Jerusalem.
7At Hebron dwelt priests of a lower degree; in Juta, those of a higher rank.
8Zachary seemed to be the Superior of them all.
9He and Elizabeth were regarded with extraordinary veneration from the fact of both having descended in a direct line from the race of Aaron.
10I saw Zachary with many people of this locality, going to a little property that he owned in the neighborhood of Juttah.
11It consisted of a house, an orchard, and a spring.
12I saw him there also with the Holy Family at the time of Mary’s Visitation.
13At the period of which I am speaking, Zachary was teaching the people and praying with them.
14It seemed to be a preparation for a feast.
15He told them of his great dejection, and of his presentiment that something remarkable was going to happen to him.
16Again I saw Zachary with the same people going to Jerusalem, where he had to wait four days before his turn to sacrifice came round.
17Until that time, he prayed in the forepart of the Temple.
18At last when his turn came, he went into the sanctuary outside the entrance to the Holy of Holies.
19The roof over the altar of incense was opened so that the sky could be seen.
20The priest offering sacrifice was not visible to those outside.
21A partition concealed him, but the smoke of the incense could be seen rising.
22I think Zachary told the other priests that he must be left alone, for I saw them leaving the sanctuary.
23Zachary went into the Holy of Holies where it was dark.
24It appeared to me that he took the Tables of the Law out of the Ark of the Covenant, and laid them upon the golden altar of incense.
25When he kindled the incense, I saw to the right of the altar a light coming down on him and in it a luminous figure.
26Zachary, frightened, stepped back and sank, as if in ecstasy, at the right side of the altar.
27The angel raised him up and spoke some words to him. Zachary replied.
28Then I saw something like a ladder let down from Heaven, and two angels ascending and descending to him.
29One took something from him; but the other—after Zachary had opened his garment—inserted a shining little body in his side.
30Zachary had become dumb.
31I saw him before leaving the Holy of Holies, writing on a little tablet that lay there.
32This tablet he sent at once to Elizabeth, who likewise had had a vision at that same hour.
33I saw that the people outside were troubled and anxious on account of Zachary’s remaining so long in the sanctuary.
34They were even moving toward the door to open it, when Zachary replaced the Tables in the Ark and came forth.
35The crowd questioned him about his long stay in the sanctuary.
36He tried to answer, but could not.
37He signified to them by signs that he had become dumb, and went away.
38Zachary was a tall and exceedingly majestic old man.


Chapter 4

THE MOST HOLY INCARNATION - Mary Espoused to St. Joseph

1Joseph was the third of six brothers. His parents dwelt in a large mansion outside of Bethlehem.
2It was the ancient birthplace of David, but in Joseph’s time only the principal walls were in existence.
3His father’s name was Jacob.
4In front of the house was a large courtyard, or garden.
5In it was a stone spring house built over a spring whose waters gushed forth out of faucets, each of which represented some animal’s head.
6The garden was enclosed by walls and surrounded by covered walks of trees and shrubbery.
7The lower story of the dwelling had a door, but no windows.
8In the upper story there were circular openings, over which ran around the whole top of the house a broad gallery with four little pavilions capped by cupolas.
9From these cupolas, a view far into the surrounding country was afforded.
10David’s palace in Jerusalem was provided with similar towers and cupolas.
11It was out of one of them that he saw Bethsabee.
12Above the center of the flat roof arose another smaller story, likewise crowned by a tower and cupola.
13Joseph and his brothers occupied that last story with an aged Jew, their preceptor.
14The latter occu-pied the highest room in the story, while the brothers slept in one chamber, their sleeping places separated from one another by mats, which in the daytime were rolled up against the walls.
15I have seen them playing up there, each in his own separate space.
16They had toys shaped like animals, like little pugs.
17Their preceptor gave them all sorts of strange instructions that I could not understand.
18He laid sticks on the ground in various figures and stood the boys in them.
19The latter stepped into other figures which they had formed by rearranging the sticks.
20They laid sticks also in various positions, as if for measurement.
21I saw too the father and mother of the boys.
22They did not appear to trouble themselves much about their children, for they paid very little attention to them.
23They, the parents, appeared to me to be neither good nor bad.
24Joseph was perhaps eight years old.
25He was very different from his brothers, very talented, and he learned quickly; but he was simple in his tastes, gentle, pious, and unambitious.
26The other boys used to play him all kinds of tricks and knock him around at will.
27They had little enclosed gardens, at whose entrance there stood on pillars covered images like swaddled infants.
28I often saw similar figures on the curtains of oratories, those of Anne and the Blessed Virgin, for instance.
29The only difference was that Mary’s picture held in its arms a chalice above which something arose.
30In Joseph’s parental home these images were like swathed infants with round faces environed by rays of light.
31There were many such pictures in Jerusalem, especially in the olden times, and also among the decorations of the Temple.
32I have seen them in Egypt also; and among the idols that Rachel purloined from her father, were similar figures though smaller.
33Many of the Jews had swathed puppets like them lying in little chests and baskets.
34They were intended to represent the child Moses in his little basket, and the swathing signified the binding power of the Law.
35When gazing at these figures, I used to think: The Jews honored the little image of the child Moses, but we have the images of the Child Jesus.
36In the boys’ little gardens grew bushes, small trees, and plants.
37I saw that his brothers often slyly trod down and tore up the plants in Joseph’s little garden.
38They always treated him roughly, but he bore all patiently.
39Sometimes, when kneeling in prayer in the colonnade that ran around the courtyard, his face turned to the wall, his brothers would push him over.
40Once I saw one of them, when Joseph was thus praying, kick him in the back; but Joseph appeared not to notice it.
41The other repeated his blows, until at last Joseph fell to the ground.
42Then I saw that he had been absorbed in God.
43But he did not revenge himself; he merely turned away quietly and sought another secluded spot.
44Outside and adjoining the garden wall, were some small, low dwellings.
45In them dwelt two elderly, veiled females, as is often the case near the schools.
46They were servants.
47I saw them carrying water into the house.
48The domestic arrangements were similar to those of Joachim and Anne’s house, the beds rolled up and wicker partitions before them.
49I often saw Joseph’s brothers talking with the servant maids and helping them in their work; but Joseph never interchanged words with them; he was always very reserved.
50I think there were also some daughters in the family.
51Joseph’s parents were not well-satisfied with him.
52They would have wished him, on account of his talents, to fit himself for a position in the world.
53But he was too unworldly for such aims, he had no desire whatever to shine.
54He may have been about twelve years old when I often saw him beyond Bethlehem opposite the Crib Cave, praying with some very pious, old, Jewish women.
55They had an oratory hidden in a vault.
56I do not know whether these women were relatives of Joseph or not; I think that they were connected with Anne.
57Joseph often went to them in his troubles and shared their devotions.
58Sometimes he dwelt in their neighborhood with a master car-penter, to whom he lent a helping hand.
59The carpenter taught him his trade, and Joseph found his geometry of use.
60The hostility of his brothers at last went so far that, when eighteen, Joseph fled from his father’s house by night.
61A friend, who lived outside of Bethlehem, had brought him clothes in which to make his escape.
62I saw him in Lebona carrying on carpentry.
63He worked for his living in a very poor family.
64The man supported himself by making such rough wicker partitions as those Joseph knew how to put together.
65The latter humbly assisted the family as far as he could.
66I saw him gathering wood and carrying it to the house.
67His parents, in the meantime, believed that he had been kidnapped; but his brothers discovered him, and then he was again persecuted.
68Joseph, however, would not leave the poor people nor desist from the humble occupation of which his family was ashamed.
69I saw him afterward in another place (Thanach).
70There he did better work for a well-to-do family.
71Though a small place, it had a synagogue.
72Joseph lived very piously and humbly, loved and esteemed by all.
73At last he worked for a man in Tiberias, at which place he lived alone near the water.
74Joseph’s parents were long since dead, and his brothers scattered; only two of them still dwelt in Bethlehem.
75The paternal mansion had passed into other hands, and the whole family had rapidly declined.
76Joseph was deeply pious; he prayed much for the coming of the Messiah. I noticed, too, his great reserve in the presence of females.
77Shortly before his call to Jerusalem for his espousals with Mary, he entertained the idea of fitting up a more secluded oratory in his dwelling.
78But an angel appeared to him in prayer, and told him not to do it; that, as in ancient times, the Patriarch Joseph became by God’s appointment the administrator of the Egyptian granaries, so now to him was the granary of Redemption to be wedded.
79In his humility Joseph could not comprehend the meaning of this and so he betook himself to prayer.
80At last he was summoned to Jerusalem to be espoused to the Blessed Virgin.
81There were seven other virgins who were with Mary to be dismissed from the Temple and given in marriage.
82On this account St. Anne went to Jerusalem to be with Mary, who grieved at the thought of leaving the Temple.
83But she was told that she must be married.
84I saw one of the distinguished old priests, who was no longer able to walk, borne into the Holy of Holies.
85An incense offering was enkindled.
86The priest prayed sitting before a roll of writings, and in vision his hand was placed upon that verse in the Prophet Isaias ( 11:1) in which it is written that there shall come forth a rod out of the root of Jesse and a flower shall rise up out of his root.
87Thereupon I saw that all the unmarried men in the country of the House of David were summoned to the Temple.
88Many of them made their appearance in holiday attire, and Mary was conducted to their presence.
89I saw one among them, a very pious youth from the region of Bethlehem, who had always ardently prayed to be allowed to minister to the advent of the Messiah.
90Great was his desire to wed Mary. But Mary wept; she wished not to take a husband.
91Then the high priest gave to each of the suitors a branch which was to be held in the hand during the offering of prayer and sacrifice.
92After that, all the branches were laid in the Holy of Holies with the understanding that he whose branch should blossom, was to be Mary’s husband.
93Now when that youth who so ardently desired to wed Mary found that this branch, along with all the others, had failed to blossom, he retired to a hall outside the Temple and, with arms raised to God, wept bitterly.
94The other suitors left the Temple, and that youth hurried to Mount Carmel where, since the days of Elias, hermits had dwelt.
95He took up his abode on the mount, and there spent his days in prayer for the coming of the Messiah.
96I saw the priests, after this, hunting through different rolls of writing in their search for another descendant of the House of David, one that had not presented himself among the suitors for Mary’s hand.
97And there they found that, among the six brothers of Bethlehem, one was unknown and ignored.
98They sought him out and so discovered Joseph’s retreat, six miles from Jerusalem, near Samaria.
99It was a small place on a little river.
100There Joseph dwelt alone in a humble house near the water, and carried on the trade of a carpenter under another master.
101He was told to go up to the Temple. He went, accordingly, arrayed in his best.
102A branch was given him. As he was about to lay it upon the altar, it blossomed on top into a white flower like a lily.
103At the same time I saw a light like the Holy Spirit hovering over him.
104He was then led to Mary, who was in her chamber, and she accepted him as her spouse.
105The espousals took place, I think, upon our 23rd of January.
106They were celebrated in Jerusalem, on Mount Zion in a house often used for such feasts.
107The seven virgins that were to leave the Temple with Mary, had already departed.
108They were recalled to accompany Mary on her festal journey to Nazareth, where Anne had already prepared her little home.
109The marriage feast lasted seven or eight days.
110The women and the virgins, companions of Mary in the Temple, were present, also many relatives of Joachim and Anne, and two daughters from Gophna.
111Many lambs were slaughtered and offered in sacrifice.
112I have had a clear vision of Mary in her bridal dress. She wore a colored, woolen under dress with-out sleeves, her arms encircled by white, woolen fillets.
113On the breast and as high as the neck, lay a white collar ornamented with jewels, pearls, etc.
114Then came a kind of gown open in front, wide like a mantle from top to bottom, and with flowing sleeves.
115This gown was blue, embroidered with large red, white, and yellow roses and green leaves, something like the ancient vestments worn at Mass.
116It fastened around the neck on the white collar, and the lower border was edged with fringes and tassels. Over this was a kind of scapular of white-and gold-flowered silk, set over the breast with pearls and shining stones.
117It lay upon the front opening of the dress, and reached to the edge of the same; it was about one-half an ell wide and was fringed with tassels and balls.
118A corresponding strip hung down the back, while shorter and narrower ones fell over the shoulders and arms.
119These lappets were caught under the arms from front to back with the gold cords, or delicate chains, with which the broad upper piece of the bodice was fastened, as also the breast piece that was placed over the upper body.
120By this arrangement, the flowered stuff of the dress was puffed out between the cords.
121The wide sleeves were tightly fastened in the middle of the upper and the lower arm by buckles, puffing out around the shoulders, the elbows, and the wrists.
122Over this costume fell a long sky-blue mantle.
123It was fastened at the neck by an ornament, and over it was a white ruffle seemingly of feathers or silk dots.
124The mantle fell back from the shoulders, forming a large fold on the sides, and hung behind in a pointed train.
125It was embroidered around the edge in flowers of gold.
126Mary’s hair was arranged with such skill as is difficult to describe.
127It was parted on top of the head and divided into numerous fine strands, which were caught together with pearls and white silk.
128It formed a large net that fell over the shoulders and down the back to the middle of the mantle. It looked like a web.
129The ends of the hair were rolled in, and the whole net edged with fringe and pearls.
130On her head was placed, first a wreath of white raw silk or wool, closing on top with three bands of the same meeting in a tuft.
131On this rested a crown about the breadth of one’s hand, set with many colored jewels.
132Three pieces arose from the circlet and met together in the center, where they were surmounted by a ball.
133In her left hand Mary carried a little garland of red and white roses made of silk, and in the right a beautiful candlestick covered with gold.
134It had no foot, but was furnished like a scepter with knobs above and below the point at which it was to be grasped by the hand.
135The stem began to swell out in the middle and ended in a little dish upon which burned a white flame.
136On her feet she wore heavy sandals about two fingers in thickness under which, before and behind, was a support like a heel.
137They were green, and gave the foot the appearance of standing upon sods.
138Two straps, white and gold, went over the foot and held them in their place.
139The virgins at the Temple arranged Mary’s skillfully woven hairnet.
140I saw them thus engaged.
141There were many busied with it, and the work went more swiftly than one could imagine.
142Anne brought all the beautiful clothes, but Mary was so modest that it was only with reluctance that she allowed herself to be arrayed in them.
143After the nuptial ceremony, her braided hair was wound around her head, a milk-white veil reaching up to the elbows thrown over her, and the crown placed upon it.
144The Blessed Virgin had auburn hair, dark eyebrows, fine and arched, a very high forehead, large downcast eyes with long, dark lashes, a straight nose, delicate and rather long, a lovely mouth around which played a most noble expression, and a pointed chin.
145She was of medium height, and she moved very gently and gravely, looking very bashful in her rich attire.
146After the marriage feast, she wore another dress.
147It was striped and less magnificent than the one described. I have a scrap of it among my relics.
148This striped dress she wore at Cana and on other holy occasions.
149She wore her wedding suit once again in the Temple.
150The very wealthy among the Jews changed their dress three or four times during a marriage feast.
151Mary in her magnificent apparel presented an appearance somewhat similar to the richly adorned women of a much later period, the Empress Helena, for instance, and even Cunegundis herself.
152The usual clothing of the Jewish women enveloped them closely, giving them an appearance of being wrapped up; but Mary’s wedding dress was very different; it was something on the Roman style.
153Joseph wore a long, wide blue coat fastened from the breast down with loops and buttons.
154The wide sleeves were laced at the sides, a broad cuff turned up at the wrist, the inside provided, as it were, with pockets.
155Around the neck was something like a brown collar, over which lay a kind of stole, and upon the breast hung two white bands.
156After the marriage, Joseph went to Bethlehem on some business, and Mary with twelve or fifteen women and maidens went to Anne’s house near Nazareth.
157They made the journey on foot.
158When Joseph returned, I saw at Anne’s house a feast at which, besides the usual household, there were about six guests and several children present. Cups were on the table.
159The Blessed Virgin wore a mantle embroidered with red, white, and blue flowers.
160Her face was covered with a transparent veil over which was a black one.
161I afterward saw Joseph and Mary in the house of Nazareth. Joseph had a separate apartment in the front of the house, a three-cornered chamber this side of the kitchen.
162Both Mary and Joseph were timid and reserved in each other’s presence.
163They were very quiet and prayerful.
164Once I saw Anne making preparations to go to Nazareth. Under her arm she carried a bundle that contained some things for Mary.
165To reach Nazareth, which lay in front of a hill, she had to go over a plain and through a grove.
166Mary wept very much when Anne was leaving and accompanied her a part of the way.
167Joseph was alone in his apartment in the front of the house.
168Mary and Joseph had, properly speaking, no regular housekeeping affairs; they received from Anne all that they needed.
169I saw Mary spinning and sewing too, but yet with wide stitches.
170The clothes then worn had not many seams and were entirely in strips. I saw her embroidering also, and with little white sticks knitting or working.
171The cooking she did was very simple and, while it was going on, the bread was baking in the ashes.
172They used sheep’s milk, and of meat generally pigeons only.

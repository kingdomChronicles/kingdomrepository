The Tragic Cycle of Sin and Humanities Approval

Romans 1:32 serves as a sobering conclusion to Paul's indictment of humanity's sinful nature. It reveals not only the depth of human rebellion against God but also the tragic reality that people do not merely engage in sin—they endorse it, encouraging others to do the same. Paul highlights that mankind is not sinning out of total ignorance; rather, people are fully aware of God's righteous decree that those who commit such sins deserve death, yet they persist in their wrongdoing. Worse still, they take pleasure in the moral corruption of others, reinforcing a cycle of disobedience and decay.

Paul's argument in Romans 1 builds upon the idea that when people reject God's truth, He allows them to follow their desires.

Romans 1:29-31 ESV

They are gossips, 30 slanderers, haters of God, insolent, haughty, boastful, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, 31 foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless.

However, Romans 1:32 makes a crucial point—sin is not just an individual problem; it is a collective one. When a society normalizes and celebrates sin, it accelerates its own destruction. Paul is not merely condemning those who commit these sins but also those who encourage them, making themselves complicit in rebellion against God.

Yet, this passage is not only about "other people." It is easy to read Romans 1 and assume Paul is referring solely to unbelievers, but in the next chapter (Romans 2:1), he turns the focus inward, showing that all humanity is guilty before God. Even those who claim to live under the law of Moses or any moral standard still fall short. Sin is not just about external actions; it is rooted in the very nature of humanity. Whether through open defiance or silent approval, every person shares in this guilt.

This reality leads us to a humbling conclusion: apart from God's grace, we are all trapped in sin. Paul’s message is not simply to expose sin but to highlight our need for redemption. Romans 1:32 should not only make us recognize the depth of human depravity but also point us toward the only hope we have—Jesus Christ. The condemnation of sin is real, but so is the invitation to salvation. Through Christ, we can break free from both the practice and approval of sin, turning instead to a life that honors God and His righteousness.

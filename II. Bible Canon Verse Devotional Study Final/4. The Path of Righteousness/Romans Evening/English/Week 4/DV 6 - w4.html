Romans 1:26-32

ESV (English Standard Version)

26 For this reason God gave them up to dishonorable passions. For their women exchanged natural relations for those that are contrary to nature; 27 and the men likewise gave up natural relations with women and were consumed with passion for one another, men committing shameless acts with men and receiving in themselves the due penalty for their error. 28 And since they did not see fit to acknowledge God, God gave them up to a debased mind to do what ought not to be done. 29 They were filled with all manner of unrighteousness, evil, covetousness, malice. They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit, maliciousness. They are gossips, 30 slanderers, haters of God, insolent, haughty, boastful, inventors of evil, disobedient to parents, 31 foolish, faithless, heartless, ruthless. 32 Though they know God’s righteous decree that those who practice such things deserve to die, they not only do them but give approval to those who practice them.

God's Judgment and Human Rebellion

Romans 1:27 is a powerful and deeply reflective verse that addresses humanity's deviation from God's design for relationships and morality. The Apostle Paul, in his letter to the Romans, speaks of a humanity that has rejected the truth of God and exchanged it for lies, leading to moral and spiritual corruption. This verse, in particular, highlights the consequences of such choices, revealing both the reality of God's wrath and the hope for redemption.

Paul’s argument in Romans 1 centers on humanity’s willful rejection of God as Creator and Sustainer. Instead of acknowledging and worshiping God, people have turned to idolatry, worshiping created things rather than the Creator.

Romans 1:18-23 ESV
18 For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who by their unrighteousness suppress the truth. 19 For what can be known about God is plain to them, because God has shown it to them. 20 For his invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made. So they are without excuse. 21 For although they knew God, they did not honor him as God or give thanks to him, but they became futile in their thinking, and their foolish hearts were darkened. 22 Claiming to be wise, they became fools, 23 and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images resembling mortal man and birds and animals and creeping things.

This refusal to honor God has profound consequences. One of the ways God expresses His judgment is by "giving humanity over" to their sinful desires. This does not mean God causes sin, but rather that He removes His restraining grace, allowing people to experience the full weight of their choices.

Romans 1:27 focuses on a specific consequence of this judgment: the distortion of human sexuality. Paul describes men abandoning natural relations with women and being consumed with passion for one another, engaging in acts that dishonor their bodies. This deviation from God's design for sexuality—intended to be expressed within the covenant of marriage between a man and a woman—illustrates humanity's descent into moral chaos when separated from God. Paul’s words highlight that such actions are not only against God’s design but also carry inherent consequences, described as a "due penalty" within themselves.

The phrase "due penalty" has been subject to much interpretation. While Paul does not explicitly define it, it likely refers to both the physical and spiritual consequences of such behaviors. These may include physical ramifications, emotional turmoil, and spiritual alienation from God. However, it is essential to note that Paul’s intent is not to single out one sin above others but to demonstrate how far humanity has strayed from God’s original purpose.

In Paul’s time, sexual sin, including homosexuality, was not only prevalent but often celebrated, particularly in pagan religious practices. Idolatry and immoral behavior were deeply intertwined in the worship of false gods, making such acts culturally normalized. Paul’s message, however, transcends cultural norms and points to a universal truth: humanity, apart from God, is in rebellion and desperately in need of redemption.

Despite the sobering reality of God’s wrath and judgment, Paul’s ultimate goal in Romans is not condemnation but salvation. He is building a case to show that all have sinned and fallen short of God’s glory, but through faith in Jesus Christ, forgiveness and restoration are available to everyone.

1 Corinthians 6:9-11 ESV
9 Or do you not know that the unrighteous will not inherit the kingdom of God? Do not be deceived: neither the sexually immoral, nor idolaters, nor adulterers, nor men who practice homosexuality, 10 nor thieves, nor the greedy, nor drunkards, nor revilers, nor swindlers will inherit the kingdom of God. 11 And such were some of you. But you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ and by the Spirit of our God.

Paul reminds believers that many of them were once trapped in various sins, including sexual immorality, but they were washed, sanctified, and justified in Christ. This serves as a powerful reminder that no sin is beyond God’s grace.

Romans 1:27, therefore, is not merely a condemnation of specific actions but part of a larger narrative about the consequences of rejecting God and the hope of redemption through Christ. It reminds us of the seriousness of sin and the depths of God’s mercy. While the world often normalizes and even celebrates behaviors contrary to God’s design, the gospel offers a different path—a call to repentance, restoration, and transformation through the power of God’s love.

In conclusion, Romans 1:27 challenges us to reflect on the consequences of turning away from God and embracing sinful desires. At the same time, it points to the hope that lies in Christ, who offers forgiveness and a restored relationship with the Creator. Paul’s message is ultimately one of grace: no matter how far we have fallen, God’s love and redemption are available to all who turn to Him in faith.

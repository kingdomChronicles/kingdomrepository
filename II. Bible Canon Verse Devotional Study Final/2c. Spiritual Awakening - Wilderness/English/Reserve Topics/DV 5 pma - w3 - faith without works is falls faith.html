James 2:14

AMP (Amplified Version)

14 What is the benefit, my fellow believers, if someone claims to have faith but has no [good] works [as evidence]? Can that [kind of] faith save him? [No, a mere claim of faith is not sufficient—genuine faith produces good works.]


GUIDE REFLECTION:


Read James 2:14-26 to better understand our study.

This verse begins a controversial passage that is often misunderstood. It is not in conflict with what Paul says in Ephesians: "For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast."

However, James is addressing a different point. It's important to note that in the original Greek, James uses the phrase hē pistis for "that faith." The word hē is crucial because it narrows down what James is referring to. In this case, it points back to the previous phrase. James is talking about a person who "says he has faith but does not have works." James then questions whether that kind of faith—one that doesn't result in good works—can save. This is why most translations use "this faith" or "such faith." James is questioning a faith that is not backed by the evidence of being saved.

James argues that genuine faith in Christ leads to genuine changes in a believer's actions. People generally act on what they truly believe. Those who believe God is right, good, and powerful will naturally obey Him. Those who believe God has called them to help others will naturally seek ways to do so. Genuine faith produces a changed life and obedience to God's will.

In the following verses, it will become clear that James's point complements what Paul says. James never suggests that we are saved by our works alone. This verse does not question the saving value of faith but the saving value of "so-called faith"—a mere belief that doesn't produce good works. James's criticism is aimed at a belief in God that doesn't result in a changed life.

It's essential to see the full context of James's message. He is not questioning all definitions of "faith" or disputing that faith alone saves. He is not claiming that works save. James is saying that saving faith, by its very nature, produces works and transformation for the better. As later verses will show, works are evidence of salvation, not the source of it.

Two critical verses for understanding this point are verse 19 "So too, faith, if it does not have works [to back it up], is by itself dead [inoperative and ineffective]." and verse 26 "For just as the [human] body without the spirit is dead, so faith without works [of obedience] is also dead." James clearly describes how mere mental "belief" is not the same as saving faith. We cannot claim salvation with our lips only; it should always result in a transformation of our old selves. A faith without works is as dead as a body without breath.

So if you say you have faith but there is no change in your life, you are among those deceived by Satan.

f you claim to have faith and say you love God but do not value reading and reflecting on His Word, your faith is dead and a lie. Your faith is fake, and you have been deceived by Satan.

We often have a misguided understanding of faith, believing in a version that is actually a fabrication of Satan. This false faith makes people think they are connected with God, when in reality, they are not. It is better to hear or read warnings now so we can understand the full truth before it is too late. Those who are deceived by Satan, along with Satan himself, will eventually be cast into the Eternal Lake of Fire.

We cannot be saved by our good works because salvation is a gift from God. Similarly, faith without accompanying actions is dead, as good works are evidence of the new life of someone with genuine faith.
